📘 Donald S. Passman — All You Need to Know About the Music Business

Key insights from the bible of music business — translated for Indian music law context and your consulting practice. Every insight ends with how YOU use it to get and serve clients.

About the book: Written by top US entertainment lawyer Donald Passman, this is the most widely read music business book globally. It is US-centric — but the underlying deal structures, royalty logic, and business principles are universal. Your job is to understand the global model and translate it to Indian law. That combination makes you exceptional.

🎵 Part 1 — The Music Business Structure
Insight 1 · The Team
Every successful artist needs five people: Manager, Lawyer, Agent, Business Manager, and PR person.
Passman explains each role clearly — the manager guides the career, the lawyer protects the rights, the agent books shows, the business manager handles money, and PR handles image. Most independent Indian artists have none of these.
🇮🇳 India Context + Your Client Angle
Independent Indian artists operate without any of this team. You can position yourself as the lawyer piece — the one person who protects their rights when they have nobody else. Your pitch: "I am the legal team most independent artists in India never have." That is your positioning statement.
Insight 2 · Record Deals
A record deal is not a gift — it is a loan against your future earnings with very unfavourable terms.
Passman breaks down how labels structure deals — advances that must be recouped, royalty rates of 15-20% of retail (not net), 360 deals taking percentage of everything. Artists sign these without understanding they may never see royalty payments even with millions of streams.
🇮🇳 India Context + Your Client Angle
Indian label deals are often even more one-sided — perpetuity clauses, all-rights assignments, no audit rights. An artist coming to sign their first label deal is your most immediate client. They need someone to read the contract before they sign. That is a ₹4,000-6,000 service you can offer right now. Post on LinkedIn: "About to sign a label deal? Read this first."
Insight 3 · Publishing is Where the Real Money Is
Passman dedicates entire chapters to publishing because it generates income long after the recording deal ends.
Publishing rights — the composition copyright — earn mechanical royalties, performance royalties, sync fees, and print royalties. A hit song earns money for 60 years after the author's death. Labels own masters. Smart artists retain publishing.
🇮🇳 India Context + Your Client Angle
In India, IPRS is the publishing collection body. Most Indian composers have no publisher and are not maximising their publishing income. Section 19A protects their royalty share even after assignment. LinkedIn post idea: "Your label owns the master. Do you still own your publishing? Most Indian artists do not know the answer."
💰 Part 2 — Royalties and Money Flow
Insight 4 · Mechanical Royalties
Every stream, download, or physical sale triggers a mechanical royalty payment to the songwriter — separate from the master recording royalty.
In the US, mechanical rates are government-set. Distributors collect and pay. Passman explains how the money flows from platform to label to artist — and where it gets stuck.
🇮🇳 India Context + Your Client Angle
India does not have a clearly defined mechanical royalty system like the US. IPRS collects performance royalties but mechanical collection is less structured. This gap is an opportunity — advising artists on how to structure their distribution agreements to capture maximum royalties is a billable service.
Insight 5 · Recoupment — The Trap
An artist can have a platinum album and still owe the label money. This is the recoupment trap.
Labels charge recording costs, marketing costs, video costs, and advances all against the artist royalty account. The artist earns 18% royalty but pays 100% of costs. Result: massive streaming numbers, zero cheque. Passman explains this with brutal clarity.
🇮🇳 India Context + Your Client Angle
Indian label contracts have the same recoupment structure. When reviewing a label deal, immediately identify: what costs are chargeable to the artist account, what the royalty base is (retail vs net), and whether the recoupment rate matches the royalty rate. This analysis alone is worth ₹5,000 to any artist about to sign.
Insight 6 · Sync Licensing is the Best Income
A sync placement in a major film or TV show can pay more than years of streaming royalties combined.
Passman explains how sync deals work — the music supervisor, the licence fee negotiation, the difference between background use and title sequence use, and why owning your masters matters so much for sync income.
🇮🇳 India Context + Your Client Angle
Indian OTT — Netflix India, Amazon Prime, JioHotstar — is booming. Music supervisors are actively licensing independent Indian music. An artist who controls their masters and composition is in a position to earn ₹50,000-5,00,000 per sync placement. Your service: structure their rights correctly so they can say yes to these deals without legal complications.
🎤 Part 3 — Live Performance and the 360 Deal
Insight 7 · Live is Now the Primary Income
In the streaming era, live performance has become the primary income source for most artists. Recordings now serve as marketing for the live show.
Passman wrote earlier editions when album sales were primary. Updated editions acknowledge that streaming commoditised recordings and live income became king. Touring artists earn through guarantees, ticket splits, and merchandise.
🇮🇳 Your Situation — Parth Specifically
You are already in the strongest position — 9 years of live experience and a built crowd in Hyderabad. Your gig income in Pune is your survival income while legal consulting grows. Do not underestimate this. The Westin crowd you built — those people are referrals, testimonials, and future clients for your legal work too.
Insight 8 · The 360 Deal — Understand Before You Sign
A 360 deal gives the label a percentage of everything — not just recordings but touring, merchandise, endorsements, acting income, and more.
Labels justify this by saying they invest in the artist's entire career. The reality is artists give up 15-25% of all income streams in exchange for the initial deal and investment. Passman calls this one of the most significant shifts in the modern music business.
🇮🇳 India Context + Your Client Angle
Indian labels are increasingly using 360 deal structures especially for newer artists. When reviewing any contract, identify 360 provisions immediately. An artist who does ₹5L in live gigs per year and signs a 360 deal gives away ₹75,000-1,25,000 annually to a label. That calculation alone justifies your review fee.
🔑 Passman's Core Principles — Applied to Your Practice
Never sign without reading
Every artist who contacts you has probably already signed something they did not understand. That is your entry point.
Own your masters if possible
Passman repeats this throughout. In India, advising artists to self-release before signing is often the right call.
Understand before you negotiate
You cannot negotiate what you do not understand. Your value is translating complex deals into plain language for artists.
Publishing outlasts everything
Masters depreciate. Publishing compounds. Help clients protect their publishing rights above all else.
The bottom line for your consulting practice
Passman's book is US law. But every problem he describes — artists losing publishing, signing away masters in perpetuity, not understanding recoupment, missing sync opportunities — exists identically in India. You are the person who understands both the global model and the Indian legal framework. That combination is rare and valuable. Use it.
📅 3-Month Study Plan — Indore to Pune Ready

Built specifically for you — knowledge that directly connects to getting clients, serving them well, and building consistent ₹50K/month income by the time you move to Pune.

The rule for this plan
Every topic has one question: "How does knowing this help me get or serve a client?" If a topic does not answer that question, it is not in this plan. You are not studying for an exam. You are studying to build a practice.
🗓️ Month 1 — Foundation and First Clients

Goal: Deep enough knowledge to confidently review a basic music contract and advise on IPRS/PPL. First paid client by end of month.

Week Topic What to Study Client Use Content Idea
Week 1 Copyright Act — Sections 13, 14, 17 Read bare act on IndianKanoon. What works are protected, what rights they carry, who owns them first. Answer: "Do I own my song?" — the most common first question from any artist client. "A single song has 3 separate copyrights. Most artists don't know this."
Week 2 Assignment and Licensing — Sections 18, 19, 30 Difference between assignment and licence. What makes an assignment valid. How sync licences work. Review any distribution or label agreement. Identify missing Section 19 elements immediately. "Signing a music contract? Check these 4 things first or it may not even be valid."
Week 3 IPRS and PPL — How Indian PROs work IPRS registration process, what they collect, how to file a claim. PPL for master owners. Section 31D broadcast licence. Audit any client's IPRS registration. Check if they are actually collecting. Recover back royalties via Section 19A. "Are you registered with IPRS? Have you ever received a payment? Most haven't. Here's why."
Week 4 Section 19A + Contract Review Practice Deep dive Section 19A. Take 2-3 sample contracts and review them applying Sections 18, 19, 19A. Write your findings in plain language. Offer first paid contract review at ₹2,500. Use the Passman + Section 19A knowledge. Deliver a 1-page plain-language findings report. "Your label owes you royalties they never paid. Section 19A of the Copyright Act explains why."
🗓️ Month 2 — Sync, Content ID and Growing Clients

Goal: Handle sync deals, Content ID disputes, and distribution agreement reviews. 2-3 paying clients. ₹15,000-25,000 income.

Week Topic What to Study Client Use Content Idea
Week 5 Sync Licensing — Indian OTT focus Master use vs sync licence. Tips v Wynk case. How Netflix India, Amazon Prime license music. Two-clearance requirement. Draft sync licence agreements. Advise artists on clearing their music for OTT placement. ₹3,000-5,000 per deal. "A web series wants to use your song. Here are the 2 licences you need before you say yes."
Week 6 YouTube Content ID — disputes and claims How Content ID works technically. Wrongful claim process. Section 51 infringement application. Dispute filing steps. Handle Content ID disputes for artists. ₹2,000-3,500 per case. Fastest way to demonstrate immediate value. "YouTube wrongly claimed your music? Here is exactly how to dispute it step by step."
Week 7 Distribution Agreements — deep review DistroKid, TuneCore, RouteNote, label distributor agreements. What clauses to flag. Ownership retention vs assignment. Territory and term. Review distribution agreements for independent artists before they sign. Most common first paid service for indie artists. "Before you upload to DistroKid — read this clause in their terms."
Week 8 Passman — Publishing and Global Royalties Read Passman chapters on publishing, mechanical royalties, and international collection. Map to Indian context — IPRS, PPL, and what Indian artists miss globally. Advise diaspora and global artists on India rights. Position as the India expert for international music lawyers and managers. "Indian artists are releasing music globally but collecting royalties in only one country. Here's what they're missing."
🗓️ Month 3 — Retainers, Global Clients and Pune Ready

Goal: First retainer client. Handle management and collaboration agreements. Approach global and diaspora clients. ₹40,000-60,000 income. Move to Pune with momentum.

Week Topic What to Study Client Use Content Idea
Week 9 Management and 360 Deals Artist management agreements. Commission structures. 360 deal provisions. What a manager can and cannot do legally. Passman chapters on managers. Review management agreements. Advise artists before signing with a manager. ₹3,000-5,000 per review. "Your manager wants 20% of everything including your live gigs. Is that legal in India?"
Week 10 Performer Rights + Moral Rights Section 38A deep dive. Section 57 moral rights. How session artists lose rights. How to draft performer consent clauses. Neha Bhasin case. Advise session musicians and vocalists. This is an underserved niche — vocalists especially need this advice and you understand them as a singer yourself. "Session singers have legal rights most labels never tell them about. Section 38A."
Week 11 Global Music Rights — Diaspora Focus ASCAP, BMI, PRS registration. How Indian artists collect globally. How foreign artists register in India. International sync deal structures. Passman — international chapters. Target UK and US diaspora Indian artists on LinkedIn. Position as the India rights expert. ₹8,000-15,000 per international consultation. "Indian artists releasing music in the UK — are you registered with PRS? Your Indian IPRS registration is not enough."
Week 12 Build Retainer Offer + Pune Launch Review everything from Months 1-3. Identify your strongest areas. Build a retainer package — monthly rights advisory for ₹15,000-20,000 per client. Identify 3 clients from your existing relationships to pitch it to. First retainer client closes this week. This is the milestone that changes everything — recurring income that does not require finding a new client every month. "I am moving to Pune and opening my music law practice. Here is what I have been building and who I can help."
