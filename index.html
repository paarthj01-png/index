⚖️ Indian Copyright Act — Key Sections

Tap any card to expand full explanation. ★ to bookmark.

🔍
Section 13 Foundation
Works Protected by Copyright
Lists every category of work that can receive copyright in India. If your work is not in this list, it has no legal protection.
Original musical works (melody + composition), literary works (lyrics — treated as a separate copyright from the music), and sound recordings (the actual recorded track) are all separately protected. A single song can carry at least three separate copyrights: the composition, the lyrics, and the master recording — each potentially owned by different people. Understanding which copyright is being dealt in any agreement is the first and most critical question a music law consultant must ask.
★
▸ Expand
Section 14 Core
Exclusive Rights of the Copyright Owner
Copyright gives you 7 exclusive rights. Only you can exercise them or give permission to others. No permission = Section 51 infringement.
(1) Reproduction: making copies in any form — CDs, files, sampling. (2) Distribution: selling or distributing copies — Spotify, Bandcamp, physical sales. (3) Performance: performing publicly — concerts, radio, clubs. (4) Communication to public: streaming — YouTube, JioSaavn, Spotify, Reels. (5) Adaptation: creating derivatives — remixes, covers, film versions. (6) Sync: combining with visuals — films, OTT, ads, games. (7) Translation: translating lyrics to another language. Every music contract is a transaction of one or more of these rights. "All rights worldwide in perpetuity" = every right, forever. The most dangerous clause in music law.
★
▸ Expand
Section 17 Ownership
First Ownership of Copyright
The author is the first copyright owner. Critical exception: work made under employment belongs to the employer.
Independent artists always own their work unless they explicitly sign it away. Session musicians, in-house producers, and staff composers often lose their rights here without realising it. Before reviewing any label or producer agreement, always confirm your client created the work as an independent contractor, not as an employee. This single determination can change the entire legal analysis of the deal.
★
▸ Expand
Section 18 Contracts
Assignment of Copyright
How copyright is permanently transferred to another person or label. Can be for specific rights, territories, and time periods.
Assignment transfers ownership — unlike a licence which only gives permission. Copyright can be assigned fully or partially. Future works can also be assigned in advance (extremely dangerous — the artist gives away work that does not yet exist). This is the legal mechanism behind every label deal. The most dangerous combination: "all rights, worldwide, in perpetuity, including future works." Each element of this should be negotiated separately.
★
▸ Expand
Section 19 Contracts
Mode of Assignment — Validity Requirements
An assignment is only valid if it is in writing, signed, and specifies rights, territory, duration, and royalties.
Oral music deals are void under Indian law. Your contract review checklist starts here — four elements must be present: (1) In writing and signed by the assignor. (2) Rights assigned must be specified. (3) Territory must be stated. (4) Duration and royalties payable must be stated. If any of these are missing, the assignment is legally incomplete and can be challenged. Many informal WhatsApp-based deals in the Indian music industry fail this test.
★
▸ Expand
Section 19A Royalties
Compulsory Royalty Sharing — Authors
Composers and lyricists always get an equal royalty share when their work is used in films or media. This cannot be waived.
This is the most violated section in Indian music contracts. When a composer or lyricist assigns copyright to a label or producer, they are still legally entitled to receive an equal share of royalties whenever the work is used in films, web series, or other media. Labels cannot override this — it is a statutory right that survives any contractual clause to the contrary. Most artists registered with labels before 2012 may be owed significant back royalties. Auditing older agreements for Section 19A violations is one of the highest-value services a music law consultant can offer.
★
▸ Expand
Section 30 Licensing
Licences by Copyright Owners
How music is temporarily permitted for use without transferring ownership. Covers sync, streaming, broadcast, and all commercial use.
Unlike assignment, a licence keeps ownership with the artist while allowing another party to use the work under specific conditions. Licences can be exclusive or non-exclusive, limited by territory, duration, and type of use. Every sync placement, streaming deal, and broadcast arrangement runs on Section 30. Always structure sync deals as licences — the artist retains ownership and can licence the same track to others simultaneously (unless the licence is exclusive). A non-exclusive licence in 3 different films is three times the income.
★
▸ Expand
Section 31 Licensing
Compulsory Licence — Withheld Works
The government can force a licence if a copyright owner wrongly withholds a work from the public.
Rarely litigated but extremely powerful as a negotiation tool. When a label is blocking release of a completed recording, refusing to revert rights, or sitting on a catalogue without exploiting it, mentioning a compulsory licence application to the Intellectual Property Appellate Board changes the negotiation dynamic immediately. The mere possibility of forced licensing often moves stuck deals forward. This is leverage, not a regular practice.
★
▸ Expand
Section 31D Royalties
Statutory Licence for Broadcasting
Radio and TV broadcasters can use sound recordings without direct permission if they notify the rights holder and pay fixed royalties.
This is why PPL exists. Broadcasters give prior notice and pay at rates fixed by the Copyright Board — they do not need the rights holder's explicit consent. If your client's music is on FM radio or TV and they are not receiving royalties, the problem is not infringement — it is PPL non-registration. Understanding this prevents unnecessary legal notices and correctly directs the client to register with PPL for collection.
★
▸ Expand
Section 38A Performers
Performer's Rights
Singers and musicians have independent rights in their performances lasting 50 years — separate from composition and master rights.
A vocalist or musician who performs has rights in that performance independently of who owns the composition or the master recording. They can object to unauthorised reproduction, broadcasting, or public communication of their performance. Session musicians and backing vocalists are almost never told about this right. Labels routinely use session performances without separate performer clearance. Always draft separate performer consent provisions in recording agreements, and advise session artists that they have rights under this section.
★
▸ Expand
Section 51 Infringement
When Copyright is Infringed
Using any Section 14 right without permission or a valid licence = copyright infringement under Indian law.
Covers all unauthorised uses: YouTube Content ID wrongful claims, unlicensed sync in ads or web series, music in Instagram Reels, background music in YouTube videos, unauthorised streaming. Also covers permitting a venue to be used for an infringing performance (relevant for live event organisers). Section 51 is the opening section of every cease and desist letter and legal notice you will ever draft for a client. Always cite the specific Section 14 right that was infringed alongside Section 51.
★
▸ Expand
Section 55 Infringement
Civil Remedies for Infringement
Three remedies: injunction (stop the use), damages (compensation), and account of profits (infringer pays over their gains).
Account of profits is the most powerful remedy — it means a brand that earned ₹50L from a campaign using unlicensed music can be ordered to hand over the profit attributable to the music, not just pay nominal damages. A strongly worded legal notice citing Sections 51 + 55 together, with specific remedies quantified, resolves the vast majority of infringement situations before any court filing. Most parties pay or remove the content rather than face litigation. This is your primary enforcement tool as a non-litigation consultant.
★
▸ Expand
Section 57 Moral Rights
Author's Moral Rights
Author retains reputation rights permanently — even after assigning all copyright. Cannot be waived or contracted away.
Even after assigning every copyright to a label, an author can still (1) claim authorship of the work, and (2) object to any distortion, mutilation, or modification of the work that damages their reputation. These rights survive any assignment and cannot be overridden by contract. Flag any waiver-of-moral-rights clause in label deals — such clauses are legally questionable under Section 57. This section also protects artists whose music is placed in contexts they find objectionable.
★
▸ Expand
Section 63 Infringement
Criminal Liability for Infringement
Wilful copyright infringement is a criminal offence — 6 months to 3 years imprisonment and fines up to ₹2,00,000.
This applies to knowing, wilful infringement — music piracy, commercial use of unlicensed music in a business context, selling unauthorised copies. As a non-litigation consultant, you will not prosecute criminal cases. However, knowing Section 63 exists allows you to advise clients on when a situation warrants escalating beyond a civil notice — which is a significant deterrent that changes the behaviour of commercial infringers who use music in advertising or events.
★
▸ Expand
📖 Music Law & Business Glossary
🔍
360 DealContracts
Label contract where the label takes a percentage of all artist revenue — recordings, touring, merchandise, endorsements, and more. Very unfavourable to artists.
AdvanceBusiness
Money paid upfront by a label, recouped from future royalties before the artist receives any net payment. A large advance with an unfavourable recoupment rate is often a trap.
ASCAPPROs
American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers. Major US PRO collecting performance royalties for composers globally.
AssignmentContracts
Permanent transfer of copyright ownership from one party to another. Unlike a licence, an assignment gives away ownership entirely and indefinitely.
Blanket LicenceLicensing
A single licence covering an entire music catalogue for a specific use, rather than licensing track by track. Common in streaming platform deals.
BMIPROs
Broadcast Music Inc. Major US PRO collecting performance royalties. Indian artists with international releases may register here alongside IPRS.
BreakageRoyalties
Royalty income retained by a label through rounding differences in calculations. A historically used mechanism to hold back a small percentage of artist earnings.
Co-publishingPublishing
Agreement where a songwriter shares publishing rights and income with a publisher, typically 50/50 of the publisher's share.
Compulsory LicenceLicensing
A licence granted by law allowing use of a copyrighted work without the owner's consent, at government-set royalty rates. See Section 31 of the Indian Copyright Act.
Content IDDigital
YouTube's automated copyright identification system. Rights holders register their content; the system identifies matching videos and allows monetisation, tracking, or blocking.
CopyrightLaw
Legal protection given to original creative works granting the creator exclusive rights to use, reproduce, distribute, and licence the work. Automatic upon creation and fixation in India — no registration required.
DAWIndustry
Digital Audio Workstation. Software used to record, edit, and produce music. Saving a project in a DAW satisfies the fixation requirement for copyright under Section 13.
Distribution AgreementContracts
Contract between an artist or label and a distributor to deliver music to streaming platforms and stores. Read carefully for territory, duration, and royalty terms.
DistroKidDistribution
Popular DIY music distribution service. Independent artists pay an annual fee to distribute music to Spotify, Apple Music, and others. Artist retains 100% of royalties.
Exclusive LicenceLicensing
A licence giving one party the sole right to use the work in a specific way, preventing even the copyright owner from granting the same rights to others during the licence term.
Fair DealingLaw
Indian equivalent of "fair use" (Section 52, Copyright Act). Allows limited use of copyrighted material for research, criticism, reporting, or education without permission.
FixationLaw
The requirement that a work be recorded in some material form to qualify for copyright protection. Writing lyrics, recording audio, or saving a DAW file all constitute fixation.
Flat FeeLicensing
A one-time payment for use of a work with no ongoing royalties. Common in sync licensing for smaller placements. Always consider the long-term value before accepting a flat fee.
IPRSPROs
Indian Performing Right Society. Primary PRO in India collecting performance royalties for composers and lyricists when their music is publicly performed, broadcast, or streamed.
ISRCTechnical
International Standard Recording Code. Unique identifier for each individual sound recording. Essential for royalty tracking across streaming platforms. Assigned by distributors.
ISWCTechnical
International Standard Musical Work Code. Unique identifier for a musical composition (not the recording). Used in publishing administration and PRO registration.
Joint WorkOwnership
Work created by two or more authors with the intention their contributions merge into a single work. Each co-author owns an undivided share of the whole copyright.
Label DealContracts
Contract between an artist and a record label covering recording, distribution, marketing, and royalties. Can be a full rights deal, licensing deal, or distribution-only deal.
LicenceLicensing
Permission to use a copyrighted work under specific conditions without transferring ownership. Can be exclusive or non-exclusive, limited by territory, time, and type of use.
Master RightsOwnership
Copyright in the specific sound recording — the actual recorded version of a song. Usually owned by the label or the artist if self-released.
Master Use LicenceLicensing
Permission to use a specific sound recording in sync with visuals. Must be obtained from the master rights owner separately from the sync licence (which covers the composition).
Mechanical RoyaltyRoyalties
Royalty paid to a songwriter or publisher each time their composition is reproduced — on a digital stream, download, CD, or vinyl. Collected by MLC in the US, PPL/IPRS in India.
Moral RightsLaw
Non-economic rights of an author to claim authorship and object to distortion of their work. Protected under Section 57 of the Indian Copyright Act. Cannot be waived or assigned.
Music PublisherIndustry
Company or individual managing a songwriter's publishing rights — registering works with PROs, collecting royalties, and licensing compositions for commercial use.
Net RevenueBusiness
Revenue after deductions. Labels often calculate artist royalties on net rather than gross revenue, significantly reducing payouts through various deductions and reserve clauses.
Non-exclusive LicenceLicensing
Permission for one party to use a work while the copyright owner retains the right to grant the same licence to others simultaneously.
OTTDigital
Over-The-Top streaming platforms delivering content via internet — Netflix, Amazon Prime, JioHotstar, Disney+. The primary sync licensing market in India as of 2026.
PerpetuityContracts
"Forever" in contract language. An assignment "in perpetuity" means no end date and no reversion. One of the most dangerous terms in any music contract.
Performance RoyaltyRoyalties
Royalty earned when music is publicly performed — broadcast on radio or TV, streamed, or played in public venues. Collected by IPRS (composition) and PPL (master) in India.
PPL IndiaPROs
Phonographic Performance Limited India. Collects and distributes performance royalties for master recording owners when sound recordings are broadcast or publicly performed.
PROPROs
Performing Rights Organisation. Body that collects and distributes performance royalties. In India: IPRS for compositions, PPL for sound recordings.
PRSPROs
PRS for Music. UK PRO collecting performance and mechanical royalties for songwriters and publishers. Indian diaspora artists in the UK often register here.
Publishing RightsOwnership
Rights in the musical composition — the melody, harmony, and lyrics. Separate from master rights. Managed by a publisher or self-administered by the artist.
RecoupmentBusiness
Process by which a label recovers its advance and expenses from an artist's earned royalties before the artist receives any net payment.
ReversionContracts
Return of copyright back to the original creator after a certain period or if specific contractual conditions are not met by the assignee. Always negotiate reversion clauses.
RoyaltyRoyalties
Payment to a rights holder for use of their copyrighted work. Types: mechanical (reproduction), performance (public use), sync (visual media), and digital (streaming).
RouteNoteDistribution
Free music distribution platform allowing independent artists to distribute music to streaming platforms. Takes a percentage of royalties on the free tier.
Sound RecordingLaw
The fixation of musical sounds in recorded form. Protected separately from the composition under Section 13 of the Indian Copyright Act. Typically owned by the label or self-releasing artist.
Sync LicenceLicensing
Permission to use a musical composition in sync with visual media — film, TV, ads, games, web series. Requires separate clearance of both the composition (sync) and the master (master use licence).
Sync RoyaltyRoyalties
Payment for using music in synchronisation with visuals. Can be a one-time flat fee or ongoing backend royalty. High-value income for artists with strong catalogues.
TermContracts
Duration of an agreement. Always negotiate a fixed term — 3, 5, or 7 years — rather than perpetuity, with clear reversion provisions at the end.
TerritoryContracts
Geographic scope of a licence or assignment. "Worldwide" means all countries. Always specify territory explicitly and consider India-only or regional deals where appropriate.
TuneCoreDistribution
DIY music distribution platform. Artists pay a per-release fee and keep 100% of their royalties. Good alternative to label distribution for independent artists.
UPCTechnical
Universal Product Code. Barcode identifier for a music release as a whole product (album or single), distinct from the ISRC which identifies individual recordings.
Work for HireOwnership
Work created under employment, where the employer is the first copyright owner under Section 17. Independent artists must ensure their work is not classified as work for hire in any agreement.

📚 Resources, Case Laws & Judgements
🔍
Case Law · Moral Rights / PRO Rights
Indian Performing Right Society v. Eastern India Motion Pictures Association (1977)
Landmark Supreme Court case establishing that IPRS has the right to collect royalties for public performance of musical works in films. Confirmed the separate rights of composers and lyricists beyond their one-time payment.
Case Law · Copyright / Composer Rights
IPRS v. Aditya Pandey (2017) — Delhi High Court
Clarified that composers and lyricists retain copyright in their individual contributions to a film, and that a film producer's copyright does not extinguish the underlying composition copyright. Critical authority for Section 19A enforcement.
Case Law · Streaming / Section 31D
Tips Industries Ltd. v. Wynk Music Ltd. (2019) — Bombay HC
Held that on-demand streaming does NOT qualify as "broadcasting" under Section 31D. OTT platforms and on-demand streaming services cannot use music under the statutory broadcast licence — they need individual licences. Major precedent for music licensing negotiations with OTT platforms.
Case Law · Online Infringement / Safe Harbour
Super Cassettes Industries v. Myspace Inc. (2016) — Delhi HC
Addressed liability of online platforms for hosting user-uploaded infringing music content. Important for understanding the extent of intermediary liability and take-down obligations for online platforms.
Case Law · Performer's Rights
Neha Bhasin v. Anand Raj Anand (2006)
One of the early Indian cases addressing a vocalist's rights under Section 38 (now Section 38A). Important precedent establishing that a singer's performance is separately protected from the underlying composition.
Case Law · Moral Rights
Arun Chopra v. Kessler Foundation (International)
Discussed the extent to which moral rights can be claimed after commercial exploitation has begun. Relevant to understanding the scope of Section 57 in practice.
Case Law · Infringement / Damages
ESPN Software India v. Tudu Enterprise (2011)
Addressed the calculation of damages in copyright infringement cases. Established that courts can award notional damages even when actual loss is difficult to quantify.
